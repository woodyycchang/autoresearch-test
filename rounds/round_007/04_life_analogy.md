Think of how a wine sommelier evaluates a single sip and produces a small, structured vocabulary of distinct descriptors — "tight tannins, bright acidity, restrained oak, lifted finish" — rather than a single 1–100 score. The same physical liquid contacts the same set of taste buds, but the trained palate decomposes the experience into a small handful of qualitative axes, each with a small number of categorical values. A scalar score loses the structure that experienced operators actually act on. A novice can be trained on the vocabulary and become useful quickly, while a scalar requires calibration against absolute standards. The taxonomy is fixed, learned through deliberate exposure to canonical examples, and recalibrated as the field evolves.
