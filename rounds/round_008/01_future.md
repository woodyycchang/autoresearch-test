In five years, agents will need a way to commit to a stable behavioral mode after extended exposure to a contextual signal — weeks of operation in a customer-specific style, months of accumulated preferences in a vertical industry, a long arc of accumulated trust with a single user — without resorting to either gradient-based fine-tuning (which is global, expensive, and discrete in time) or in-context learning (which is local and lost on session reset). The interesting design space is between those poles: probabilistic per-component commitment that integrates a long-duration signal into a population-level threshold crossing. Once committed, the mode persists across sessions; once an explicit reset event occurs (new tenant, new policy, new role), the commitment dissolves. The mechanism is slow-integrating-then-discrete-flipping rather than fast-or-instant. Evaluations will measure both the time-to-commit and the cleanliness-of-reset.
