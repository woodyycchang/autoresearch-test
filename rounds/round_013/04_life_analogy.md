Think of how a busy garage tunes a car's engine. Many small adjustments — timing, fuel mixture, idle speed — leave most of the car's behaviors unchanged: AC still works, transmission still shifts, radio still plays. But certain adjustments interact predictably with neighboring systems: a timing change near a known threshold can disturb the catalytic converter; a fuel-mixture change can throw off the oxygen sensor. A skilled mechanic knows which systems are "close" to the adjustment and which are "far"; far-systems survive untouched, close-systems must be inspected after. The principle is: most subsystems are robust to small perturbations; the fragile ones share a representational neighborhood with the perturbation.
