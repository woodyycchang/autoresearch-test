Picture a contractor running a long renovation. Early on the contractor tells the homeowner "the load-bearing wall in the kitchen will stay; we'll rework the layout around it." Subcontractors then plan electrical, plumbing, and cabinet routing using that wall as a fixed reference. Three weeks in, the contractor cannot quietly decide "actually we're removing that wall" — every downstream subcontractor's plan was built on the original commitment, and silently flipping it corrupts all their work. The professional norm is: if the original statement must be reversed, the contractor must issue an explicit retraction, identify every downstream plan that relied on it, and reissue or rework those plans openly. The cost of the retraction is roughly proportional to how many downstream things relied on the original commitment.
