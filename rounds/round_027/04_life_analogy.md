Mixing salt into a bowl of water: macroscopically the salt is "gone" once dissolved, but the molecules are still there, and a sufficiently careful microscopic process (evaporation under controlled conditions) recovers them. The Loschmidt-Boltzmann paradox in classical statistical mechanics formalizes this: microscopic laws are time-reversible (Hamiltonian dynamics), so true irreversible erasure of microstate information at the macroscale must arise from coarse-graining (loss of access), not from genuine destruction of the information. Forgetting a specific piece of information about the initial microstate is, in this sense, *impossible* unless the system is opened to an external bath and entropy is exported. By analogy, a gradient-descent-trained LLM is a deterministic dynamical system in weight space; an "unlearning" step is a small perturbation of weights; the original-information-bearing direction in weight space is not destroyed by that perturbation but only made harder to access, in the same way Loschmidt's microreversibility makes molecular forgetting only apparent.
