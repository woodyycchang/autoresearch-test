A child memorizes multiplication tables. For weeks, they recite numbers correctly on the tables they have seen and fail on new pairs. Then one week, without obvious external change, the child generalizes — they correctly multiply numbers they have never seen, applying place-value algorithms. The neural-developmental signature of this transition is a measurable delayed-consolidation phase where representations restructure. This is the biological analog of grokking. The Form-D direction asks whether LLM grokking — which is now well-instrumented — predicts a specific consolidation window in cortex.
