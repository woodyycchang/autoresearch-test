Embryo development. A fertilized egg, initially homogeneous, breaks symmetry via gradient of morphogen molecules (Bicoid in Drosophila). Cells in different positions along the gradient activate different gene programs and assume different fates. The mechanism has two coupled parts: a graded chemical signal (the morphogen field) AND a locally-bistable response (the reaction-diffusion Turing dynamic), together producing sharp boundaries between regions and stable role assignments. Neither the gradient alone nor the reaction-diffusion alone would work — gradient alone gives smooth differentiation without boundary sharpening; RD alone gives random patterns without spatial localization.
