When you look through a stack of receipts looking for the one with a specific charge, the most-relevant receipt is usually one of the first you check (if you sort by date or merchant); each successive receipt is increasingly unlikely to be the one. This decay is not exponential but power-law: there is a long tail of weakly-relevant receipts. The same shape appears in word-frequency in natural language (Zipf's law: f_k ∝ k^{-α}, α≈1), in city sizes (Zipf-Auerbach), and in many other ranking phenomena. A unifying explanation is the sample-space-collapse mechanism: as you commit to the top-ranked item, the conditional space of remaining items shrinks proportionally to its rank, giving the k^{-α} form. The retrieval-relevance-vs-rank curve, if Zipfian, would reflect the same underlying sample-space-collapse process during embedding-space search.
