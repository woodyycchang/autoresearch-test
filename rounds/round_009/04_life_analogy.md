Think of how a master chef evaluates a dish using a small, fixed set of distinct axes — salt, acid, fat, heat — each with its own baseline level appropriate to the cuisine, each correctable by a specific opposite-quality intervention if it drifts. "Too salty" is corrected by adding acid or fat, not by reducing salt. "Too rich" is corrected by adding acid, not by reducing fat. The diagnosis is always a small profile, never a scalar score, and the intervention vocabulary is opposite-quality, not subtractive. A good cook reads the small profile fast and applies the right counter-quality intervention before the dish is served.
