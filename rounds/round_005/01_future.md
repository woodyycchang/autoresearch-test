In five years, RLHF-style alignment training will operate continuously rather than as discrete fine-tuning rounds, with safety teams pushing in one direction (e.g., reducing harmful outputs on category A) and product teams pushing in another (e.g., recovering helpfulness on adjacent category not-A) on a rolling cadence. The bottleneck shifts from per-round optimization to managing asymmetric over-correction: pushing harder in one direction creates a measurable "softening" in the reverse direction, where the model over-yields on near-boundary prompts. Predicting the magnitude of this asymmetric softening before each training round will be critical to avoid oscillation. The discipline of safe iterative alignment will need a quantitative theory of "training plasticity under reverse loading" — the per-prompt elastic limit, where the elastic limit itself depends on the history of prior training pushes.
