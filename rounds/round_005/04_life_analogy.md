Think of bending a paperclip repeatedly to break it. The first bend in one direction takes real force. Bend it back to straight, and the next bend in the original direction is now easier than the first one — but bending in the opposite direction is noticeably harder than the first opposite-direction bend would have been. The metal "remembers" the prior bend in a way that asymmetrically lowers its yield in one direction and raises it in the other. After enough cycles, fatigue sets in and the paperclip snaps without much force at all. The pattern is universal across cyclic stressing: each push leaves a directional residual that asymmetrically biases the next push.
