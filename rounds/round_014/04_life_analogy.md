Picture a sponge under a running tap. Water from the tap flows across the surface, but a small fraction continually presses into the sponge, gets held briefly, transforms (picks up soap, releases air bubbles, changes temperature) and re-emerges. The exchange is bidirectional and continuous — surface water moves into the sponge in some spots, sponge-held water emerges in others — and the slow transit through the porous interior is where the actual chemistry happens. The sponge is not a separate reservoir; it is a transit zone integrated with the surface flow. The processing happens during transit, not at boundary.
