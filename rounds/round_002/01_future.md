In five years, agents running for weeks at a time will accumulate enough intermediate scratch state — partial plans, abandoned subroutines, deprecated tool wrappers, stale embeddings — that they will need an active internal "garbage" handling subsystem rather than relying on bounded context windows to silently truncate the past. Discarding too aggressively destroys hard-won context; keeping everything explodes inference cost and dilutes attention. Agents will need a tagged, sortable, partly-resource-pressure-driven mechanism that decides what to recycle, what to compress, and what to surface back into active use. The trigger for cleanup will be a real-time signal (token-budget pressure, latency budget, anomaly rate) rather than a fixed schedule. Different content types — code skills, episodic facts, environment models, transient goals — will need different recycling pathways. The metric of agent health will include the ratio of usable-to-stale internal state, not just task success.
