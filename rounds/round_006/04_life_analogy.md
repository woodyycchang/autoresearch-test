Think of how a household's wooden butcher block develops small cracks over years of use, and how each new oiling — done in response to a visible dry spot, not on a calendar — locally swells the wood fiber and closes the crack from the inside. The oil isn't applied uniformly; it's applied where the block exposes a dry patch. The block carries reserves of older oil deep in the grain, which migrate to the surface only when local drying creates a pressure gradient. Across decades the block ends up denser and more refined exactly along the surfaces that have been most stressed — repair followed the failure pattern, not a maintenance schedule. New cracks become harder to start in those refined regions, but appear in fresh untouched regions instead.
