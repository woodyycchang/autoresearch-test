By 2031, LLM-driven multi-agent systems will routinely deploy 50-500 agents coordinating on shared tasks. Coordination via explicit message passing fails to scale (O(N²) protocol overhead) and forces synchronization barriers that cap throughput. The dominant coordination substrate will become indirect state-based coordination on shared persistent artifacts, where agents observe artifact state and write incremental changes. This shift is analogous to biological collective intelligence in eusocial insects. Achieving it robustly requires solving two coupled problems: (1) ensuring coordination signals do not degrade under noisy partial-observation, (2) ensuring incremental edits accumulate in one direction (no work undo) over long runs. The current research literature has only just begun to map these requirements.
