A fly that smells a particular floral scent paired with sugar a few times will later approach that scent without sugar. The neural substrate is the mushroom body: ~2000 Kenyon cells (KCs) sparsely encode the odor; ~20 output neurons (MBONs) read out via plastic synapses modulated by dopamine. The learning algorithm at the circuit level is: a coincidence detector (KC AND dopamine signal) and a readout that biases behavior. This is a two-step circuit. Compared with the transformer induction-head algorithm (a previous-token-attention-head identifies a recent token A, then a second induction-attention-head queries 'when A appeared before, what came next? B'), there is a structural analogy: each is a two-step circuit where the first step identifies a cue and the second step retrieves a paired completion. The mapping is: KC ↔ first attention head sparsely encoding cue; MBON dopamine-plasticity ↔ induction-head's recall mechanism. The reverse-direction question is whether LLM mechanistic-interpretability discoveries can predict new fly-mushroom-body wiring or dynamics findings.
