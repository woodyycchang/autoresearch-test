Picture a nomadic herder moving animals across a landscape carved into separate valleys by mountain passes. On any given day the herder wanders within whichever valley she is currently in, and her daily route, water sources, and nightly campsite are all chosen from inside that valley's local options. To leave for a neighboring valley she has to deliberately re-cross a specific pass, usually only feasible in a specific season — outside of those transitions, her movements are confined to one valley's interior. If you only photograph her once a week from above and average her positions across a whole year, the average is dominated by whichever valleys she camped in longest, not by some smooth landscape-wide center. The interesting events are not the daily wandering but the rare pass-crossings, because each one resets which long-run average she is converging to.
