By 2031 LLM-based agents will routinely operate as long-lived autonomous "research assistants" that maintain persistent project state across weeks of work, rather than starting from scratch each conversation. They will issue thousands of tool calls per task, spanning code execution, web browsing, and physical-world interfaces, and will need to retain skill memory across heterogeneous problem distributions without catastrophic interference. Inference-time scaffolding will look less like single-pass chain-of-thought and more like a self-managed scheduler that allocates compute across exploration, exploitation, verification, and rest phases. Agents will need a reliable mechanism for deciding when a piece of internal state is "stale enough" to discard versus "load-bearing" enough to migrate forward, because their effective context will be much larger than what any single forward pass can fit. The bottleneck will shift from raw parameter count or context length to the agent's policy for what to consolidate, what to compress, and what to forget. Evaluations will become longitudinal and trajectory-based rather than benchmark-snapshot-based.
